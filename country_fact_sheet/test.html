Trade and investment between the United Kingdom and Afghanistan

Trade:

Total trade in goods and services (i.e. exports plus imports) between the UK and Afghanistan was £354m in the four quarters to the end of Q2 2020, a decrease of 20.8% or £93m from the four quarters to the end of Q2 2019. Of this £354m:

  • Total UK exports to Afghanistan amounted to £223m in the four quarters to the end of Q2 2020 (a decrease of 4.7% or £11m compared to the four quarters to the end of Q2 2019);
  • Total UK imports from Afghanistan amounted to £131m in the four quarters to the end of Q2 2020 (a decrease of 38.5% or £82m compared to the four quarters to the end of Q2 2019).

Afghanistan was the UK’s 105th largest trading partner in the four quarters to the end of Q2 2020 accounting for less than 0.1% of total UK trade.

Afghanistan was the UK’s 99th largest export market and the UK’s 105th largest import market in the four quarters to the end of Q2 2020, accounting for less than 0.1% of UK exports and less than 0.1% of UK imports.

(Trade for types of goods and services can also be found in this document)

Imports from the UK represented a market share of 4.9% of all the imports by Afghanistan in 2019.

Foreign Direct Investment (FDI):

The outward stock of foreign direct investment (FDI) from the UK in Afghanistan is not available due to data disclosure.

The inward stock of foreign direct investment (FDI) in the UK from Afghanistan was £0 thousand in 2018, accounting for less than 0.1% of the total UK inward FDI stock.

Trade (ONS)[1] [2] [3] [4]

Trade definitions

Exports - sales of goods and services by residents of the reporting partner to non-residents.

Imports - purchases of goods and services by residents of the reporting partner from non-residents.

Total or ‘bilateral’ trade - the value of total trade between two trading partners (i.e. exports plus imports).

Trade balance - the difference between exports and imports. Exports minus imports.

Trade surplus - occurs when the value of a partner’s exports is greater than that of its imports.

Trade deficit - occurs when the value of a partner’s exports is less than that of its imports.

The following figures are from the ONS and are on a Balance of Payments (‘change of ownership’) basis. [5]

Note that, the latest data include March to June 2020, during which time the UK introduced a number of measures to combat the COVID-19 pandemic, including lockdown measures between March and May 2020. The UK’s major trading partners also introduced lockdown measures during 2020 and will therefore have been significantly affected by COVID-19. The effects will be more limited in the Factsheets as data are presented on a 12 month rolling basis. Recent data may likely be subject to larger revisions than usual in the future, as data quality has also been affected.

Total trade in goods and services (i.e. exports plus imports) between the UK and Afghanistan was £354m in the four quarters to the end of Q2 2020, a decrease of 20.8% or £93m from the four quarters to the end of Q2 2019.

In the four quarters to the end of Q2 2020, total UK exports to Afghanistan amounted to £223m (a decrease of 4.7% or £11m compared to the four quarters to the end of Q2 2019).

Of all UK exports to Afghanistan in the four quarters to the end of Q2 2020, £53m (23.8%) were goods and £170m (76.2%) were services. In the four quarters to the end of Q2 2020, UK exports of goods to Afghanistan decreased by 7.0% or £4m compared to the four quarters to the end of Q2 2019 while UK exports of services to Afghanistan decreased by 4.0% or £7m compared to the four quarters to the end of Q2 2019.

In the four quarters to the end of Q2 2020, total UK imports from Afghanistan were £131m (a decrease of 38.5% or £82m compared to the four quarters to the end of Q2 2019).

Of all UK imports from Afghanistan in the four quarters to the end of Q2 2020, £3m (2.3%) were goods and £128m (97.7%) were services. In the same period, UK imports of goods from Afghanistan increased by £3m compared to the four quarters to the end of Q2 2019 while UK imports of services from Afghanistan decreased by 39.9% or £85m compared to the four quarters to the end of Q2 2019.

This means the UK reported a total trade surplus of £92m with Afghanistan, compared to a trade surplus of £21m in the four quarters to the end of Q2 2019. In the four quarters to the end of Q2 2020, the UK had a trade in goods surplus of £50m with Afghanistan, compared to a trade surplus of £57m in the four quarters to the end of Q2 2019. Meanwhile, in the four quarters to the end of Q2 2020 the UK reported a trade in services surplus of £42m with Afghanistan, compared to a trade deficit of £36m in the four quarters to the end of Q2 2019.

In the four quarters to the end of Q2 2020, Afghanistan was the UK’s[6]:

More timely data, for trade in goods only, show that UK exports of goods to Afghanistan decreased by 52.0% in the 12 months to September 2020 compared to the same period the previous year. UK imports of goods from Afghanistan increased by 100.0% over the same period.[7]

Data between March and September 2020 have been affected by the COVID-19 pandemic. As this data are presented for the 12 months ending September 2020, this also includes trade between October 2019 to February 2020 before any UK lockdown measures were introduced. Data quality has also been affected and may be subject to larger revisions than usual.

Market Share (ONS and UNCTAD)[10] [11] [12]

Market Share definitions

UK market share – Imports from the UK as a percentage of all the goods and services imported by Afghanistan. Market share is provided for total imports from the UK, as well as for goods and services separately.

Market share methodology – Market share is calculated as the value of imports from the UK using data from ONS, divided by the value of total imports using data from UNCTAD. Data from UNCTAD is converted from US dollars into pounds sterling by using the annual average spot exchange rate[13].

  • The UK market share in Afghanistan was 4.9% in 2019 for goods and services. This is an increase of 1.8 percentage points from 2018.
  • The UK market share in Afghanistan was 1.6% in 2019 for goods only. This is an increase of 1 percentage points from 2018.
  • The UK market share in Afghanistan was 20.6% in 2019 for services only. This is an increase of 5.6 percentage points from 2018.

Trade in goods by UK countries and English regions (HMRC)[14]

Regional trade definition

UK regions - The UK is categorised into 12 regions, which includes 9 regions in England, as well as Wales, Scotland and Northern Ireland. These UK regions are based on the Nomenclature of Territorial Units for Statistics references as defined by Eurostat as NUTS1 regions.

Regional trade data - The data is compiled by merging HMRC trade data with employment data from the Interdepartmental Business Register (IDBR) so a business’ trade can be allocated to a UK region based on the proportion of its employees employed in that region. Where a trader is not matched with the IDBR, its trade is matched with ONS postcode data to obtain the UK region in which the Head Office of the VAT-registered business is based.

Data for UK regions is not available for Afghanistan in 2019.

Trade in Value Added (OECD)[19]

Trade in Value Added definitions

Trade in Value Added (TiVA) - A new statistical approach for measuring trade. Unlike traditional (‘gross’) trade statistics, TiVA decomposes gross exports into domestic and foreign value added. It regards international trade not as gross flows of final goods and services but as flows of value added and identifies in which trading partners and industries value is added along the value chain. In this way, it provides a more accurate picture of international trade relationships and partners’ participation in Global Value Chains. More information on TiVA can be found in a recent research report.

Backward linkages in exports - measures how dependent domestic exporters are on foreign inputs (raw materials, machinery etc.) for the production of their exports.

Note: TiVA data for Afghanistan are not available.

UK backward linkages:

  • In 2015 (latest data), around 15.1% of the value added in UK’s gross exports reflected imports from abroad (‘foreign value added in UK’s gross exports’). The contribution of Afghanistan to the total value added in the UK’s gross exports is not available.
  • The largest source of foreign value added in UK exports were United States (2.1%), Germany (1.8%), and China (1.2%).

Official Development Assistance definition

Official Development Assistance (ODA) - ODA is defined by the OECD Development Assistance Committee (DAC) as government aid that promotes and specifically targets the economic development and welfare of developing countries and territories. A list of countries and territories that are eligible for ODA can be found on the OECD website.

Foreign Direct Investment with Afghanistan (ONS)[21] [22] [23] [24] [25]

Investment definitions

Foreign direct investment (FDI) - Investment made by an entity resident in one economy (‘direct investor’) to acquire a ‘lasting interest’ in an enterprise operating in another economy. The ‘lasting interest’ is deemed to exist if the direct investor acquires at least 10% of equity, or equivalently 10% of the voting rights, of the company.

FDI stock - the accumulated value of all previous investments at the end of a reference period. Figures are net, that is the value of investment minus disinvestment.

UK outward FDI stock refers to the total value of UK residents’ FDI abroad (investment of UK parent company in the foreign affiliate).

UK inward FDI stock refers to the total value of FDI by non-UK resident investors in the UK (investment of foreign parent company in UK affiliate).

Disclosure - It can happen that FDI to a certain partner is coming from only a few investors. In that case, figures are suppressed to avoid disclosure of information relating to individual enterprises.

UK outward FDI stock:

  • The stock of FDI from the UK in Afghanistan is not available due to data disclosure.
  • The total UK FDI abroad in 2018 was £1.4t. The top destination for UK FDI in 2018 was United States, accounting for 20.9% of the total UK outward FDI stock, followed by Netherlands (11.4%) and Luxembourg (8.3%)[26].

UK inward FDI stock:

  • The stock of FDI from Afghanistan in the UK was £0 thousand in 2018, comparison with previous year not available due to data disclosure. In 2018, Afghanistan accounted for less than 0.1% of the total UK inward FDI stock.[27]
  • The total FDI in the UK in 2018 was £1.5t. The top investor in the UK in 2018 was United States, accounting for 27.4% of the total UK inward FDI stock, followed by Netherlands (9.0%) and Jersey (7.3%)[28].

Ease of doing business (World Bank)[29]

Ease of doing business definitions

Ease of doing business ranking - Trading partners are ranked by World Bank on how easy it is to conduct business with them. A high ‘Ease of doing business’ ranking means the regulatory environment is more conducive to the starting and operation of a local firm than other partners which are ranked lower. A total of 190 partners were ranked by World Bank for this metric.

Ease of doing business score - The ease of doing business rankings are based on each partner’s ‘Ease of doing business’ score. The ease of business scores were determined by scoring each partner in 10 individual indicators, such as the ease of starting a new business and trading across borders, and then taking an average of these 10 scores. Rankings for each of these individual indicators can be found on the World Bank website. Each score is given on a scale from 0 to 100, where 100 represents the best performance. These scores can be compared between years as an increase in score from the previous year would indicate that partner is improving in their ease to do business with. For further detail, see methodology explaining how these scores are calculated.

 

Data presented here are subject to change as data irregularities were found in the latest Ease of Doing Business report: Please use statistics presented here with caution.

  • In 2019, Afghanistan was ranked 173rd for its ease of doing business. This was 6 places worse than in 2018.
  • In 2019, Afghanistan had an ease of business score of 44.1, compared to 44.2 in the previous year.
  • In 2019, the UK was ranked 8th for its ease of doing business (1 place better than in 2018), behind New Zealand, Singapore, Hong Kong SAR, Denmark, South Korea, United States and Georgia.
  • In 2019, the UK had an ease of business score of 83.5, compared to 83.6 in the previous year.

Glossary of terms

Physical movement (or customs) basis - a good is recorded as an export (import) if it physically leaves (enters) the economic territory of a trading partner. Goods data from the HMRC and UN Comtrade are reported on this basis.

Change of ownership (or Balance of Payments) basis - a good leaving (entering) the economic territory of a trading partner is recorded as an export (import) only if it has changed ownership between the resident of the reporting partner and non-residents. The ONS goods data shown in this factsheet are reported on this basis - hence they are not directly comparable with the HMRC goods data which are based on the ‘physical movement’ principle.

HMRC data are comparable with UN Comtrade goods data as they are both compiled on a physical movement basis. The data published by UN Comtrade are directly supplied by HMRC but lags in the timing of revisions by UN Comtrade and exchange rate conversions (from £ to $) mean that there might be some discrepancies between the two sources.

Trade in services involves no physical movement (services are intangible) so services data are always reported on a ‘change of ownership’ (Balance of Payments) basis.

Seasonal adjustment - The purpose of seasonal adjustment is to remove systematic calendar-related variation associated with the time of the year, that is, seasonal effects. This facilitates comparisons between consecutive time periods. Seasonal effects can be influenced by weather patterns; administrative measures; social, cultural and religious events; variation in the length of months and quarters; number of trading days in a month; and moving holidays such as Easter and Ramadan. It is advised not to compare seasonally and non-seasonally adjusted data unless one is looking at full calendar years where seasonally and non-seasonally adjusted data should agree for the same period.

Nominal (or current price) data - measure the face value of goods and services in a given time period.

Real (or constant price) data - values are adjusted for price level changes (inflation).

HS commodity code - The Harmonized System is an international nomenclature for the classification of products. It allows participating partners to classify traded goods on a common basis for customs purposes. At the international level, the Harmonized System (HS) for classifying goods is a six-digit code system. The HS comprises approximately 5,300 article/product descriptions. The six digits can be broken down into three parts. The first two digits (HS-2) identify the chapter the goods are classified in, e.g. 09 = Coffee, Tea, Mate and Spices. The next two digits (HS-4) identify groupings within that chapter, e.g. 09.02 = Tea, whether or not flavoured. The next two digits (HS-6) are even more specific, e.g. 09.02.10 Green tea (not fermented)… Up to the HS-6 digit level, all partners classify products in the same way (a few exceptions exist where some partners apply old versions of the HS). Information on Harmonized Commodity Description and Coding Systems (HS) can be found at the World Customs Organization website

SITC commodity code - The Standard International Trade Classification (SITC) Revision 4 is recognised by the UN Statistical Commission as an analytical tool for international merchandise trade statistics. SITC covers all goods classifiable in the Harmonized System except for monetary gold, gold coin and current coin. The main categories in SITC are food, drinks and tobacco (sections 0 and 1); raw materials (sections 2 and 4); energy products (section 3); chemicals (section 5); machinery and transport equipment (section 7); and other manufactured goods (sections 6 and 8). Information on SITC can be found at the UN Statistics Division website.

EBOPS product code - EBOPS2010 is an international nomenclature for the classification of service products. It allows participating partners to classify services on a common basis. It is product based classification which is consistent with but more detailed than the BPM6 classification for more details please see the Manual on Statistics of International Trade in Services 2010 - UNSD.

FDI based on directional basis - Under the directional presentation, the direct investment flows and positions are organised according to the direction of the investment for the reporting economy – either outward or inward. So, for a particular partner, all flows and positions of direct investors resident in that economy are shown under outward investment and all flows and positions for direct investment enterprises resident in that economy are shown under inward investment.

FDI based on asset/liability basis - On an asset/liability basis, direct investment statistics are organised according to whether the investment relates to an asset or a liability for the partner compiling the statistics. For example, a partner’s assets include equity investments by parent companies resident in that trading partner in their foreign affiliates because those investments are claims that they have on assets in foreign partners. Similarly, a partner’s liabilities include foreign parents’ equity investments in affiliates resident in that partner because those investments represent claims that foreigners have on assets in the reporting partner. The asset/liability presentation does not show the direction of influence as the directional presentation does.

Trade asymmetries - Discrepancies in bilateral trade statistics reported by trading partner. For example, the value of UK exports to USA (reported by the ONS) does not match the value of US imports from the UK (reported by the US Bureau of Economic Analysis, the US statistical agency). Asymmetries in trade statistics reflect a number of factors, including the application of different criteria of partner attribution in import and export statistics, valuation (imports CIF, exports FOB), differences in inclusions/exclusions of particular commodities or types of services, differences in survey methods, exchange rates, time lags etc. - see here for more detail on trade asymmetries. Asymmetries also exist in bilateral FDI statistics. Users are advised to use UK-reported data as far as possible.

FDI asymmetries - Discrepancies in bilateral FDI statistics reported by trading partners. For example, the value of UK outward FDI stock in the USA (reported by the ONS) does not match the value of inward FDI stock from the UK (reported by the US Bureau of Economic Analysis). The size of difference and reasons for these FDI asymmetries have been researched in a recent report by the ONS.
